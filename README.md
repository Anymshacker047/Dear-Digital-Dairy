Digital Diary — Investigation, Truth, and Human Nature

Today began like any ordinary morning.
It was around 10:30 AM when I was woken up unexpectedly by my family. They informed me that a girl had come to meet me regarding something urgent.

Still half-asleep and dressed casually, I stepped out—only to find a distressed young woman, visibly anxious and on the verge of tears. The situation was both awkward and serious.

After calming her down, I listened to her for nearly half an hour. She explained that her Instagram account was sending messages on its own—messages she had never written. She was confused, scared, and unaware of what was happening.

I asked her to sit with me at the system, and her father joined us as well. What initially seemed like a simple account issue soon turned into a deeper investigation.

Upon inspection, the password remained unchanged. However, inside the Meta Account Center, multiple unknown IDs were linked. I immediately removed the suspicious account and secured her profile.

But the real investigation had just begun.

There were four individuals in the house:
her elder sister (pregnant and in her final days), a young nephew, her brother-in-law, and herself.

Through logical elimination, the pregnant sister and the 8-year-old child were ruled out. The nature of the abusive messages clearly indicated involvement of an adult.

Further analysis revealed an undisclosed ID that showed suspicious login activity—specifically between 2:30 AM and 3:00 AM—from the same device. After each session, the traces were cleaned.

This pattern shifted the direction of the case. It was no longer an external hack—it was internal access.

The girl then mentioned a “trusted friend” who knew her password and had no two-factor authentication enabled. I contacted him, but his responses were inconsistent and evasive, raising further suspicion.

In the evening, I called them back with the suspected individual. His phone was thoroughly analyzed, but no direct evidence was found. However, I extended the investigation to his recent contacts, considering the possibility of indirect involvement.

Still, something felt incomplete.

So instead of rushing to conclusions, I set a trap.
The suspicious ID was left active under monitoring, and firewall protections were deployed across the system.

That night, the truth revealed itself.

At around the same time—2:30 AM—the account was accessed again. This time, the pattern confirmed everything.

The brother-in-law himself was logging into the account.
He was coordinating with a friend to send abusive messages—intentionally harassing his own sister-in-law.

It was not just a cyber issue.
It was a breach of trust within the household.

This case taught me something beyond technology:
Not every attack comes from outside.
Sometimes, the real threat exists within the circle of trust.
